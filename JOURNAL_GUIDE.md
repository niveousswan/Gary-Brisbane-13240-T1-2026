# COIT13240 Journal Guide

This guide explains how to write high-quality weekly journal entries. Read it carefully before you begin.

---

## 1. Introduction

Your journal captures your personal learning, understanding, and reflections each week. There is no single "correct" journal — entries will naturally vary between students. Keep the following in mind:

- A journal is personal. If your entry looks different from an example, that doesn't mean it's wrong.
- The unit content and assessment requirements may change each term — always align your entries to the current unit.

---

## 2. Evidence of Your Own Work

Simply saying *"I completed all tutorial tasks"* is **not sufficient**. You must **show** that you did it. Here are the accepted ways:

### 2.1 Photos and Manual Calculations
For tasks involving manual calculations (e.g., classical ciphers), take a photo or screenshot of your handwritten or drawn workings. Messy notes are fine — what matters is authenticity.

### 2.2 Screenshots of Commands and Software
Screenshots are good evidence, but they should capture **information specific to you**, such as:
- Your username or identifying information
- Date/time
- Files or addresses specific to your task

**Tips for screenshots:**
- Always explain what the screenshot shows — don't just paste it in without context.
- Don't screenshot every single step. Focus on the final result or key steps.
- For command-line work (PowerShell or Linux), you can paste commands directly into Markdown using code blocks:

  ````
  ```bash
  your command here
  ```
  ````

  See [GitHub code block docs](https://docs.github.com/en/get-started/writing-on-github/working-with-advanced-formatting/creating-and-highlighting-code-blocks).

### 2.3 Including Files (Python, OpenSSL, PCAP, etc.)
Upload files that are specific to your work and **link to them** from your journal entry. For example:
- If you wrote Python code, include the `.py` file and link to it — don't just paste a screenshot of code.
- You may include short code snippets using Markdown code blocks, but always include the actual file too.

---

## 3. Reflecting on Your Progress

Reflections are personal. There's no single correct way to write them. Here are areas to consider:

### 3.1 What Was Easy and Hard?
Don't just say *"everything was easy."* Justify yourself:
- What made it easy? Had you covered this in a previous unit?
- Did you get everything right on the first attempt? If not, why not, and how did you fix it?
- Were some tasks harder than others? Which ones, and why?
- If you genuinely found it all easy, share novel insights that go beyond what was taught.

### 3.2 How Did You Improve?
When you identify a weakness, describe the strategies you used to address it. For example:

> *I found Linux filesystem navigation hard because I had never used the command line before. I watched [this YouTube tutorial](https://...) and practised for 2 hours. I can now navigate confidently.*

Link to any resources that helped and explain how they helped.

### 3.3 Connect Current Learning to Past Work
Learning IT is progressive. Consider:
- How does this week's practical work connect to the lecture content?
- How does it relate to previous weeks?
- What knowledge or skills from prior units (or workplace experience) did you draw on? (Name the units and topics specifically.)

---

## 4. Going Beyond Unit Content

To achieve high grades (D or HD), you need to demonstrate excellent understanding — not just complete the minimum tasks. You can go beyond by:

- Reading papers, guides, news articles, or tech reports related to the topic.
- Exploring a topic in more depth than covered in the unit.
- Solving a tutorial problem in multiple ways and comparing approaches.

Always explain **why** you chose to explore something further and **how** it connects to the unit content.

> Note: Novel insights are assessed across the full journal, not on a per-week basis.

---

## 5. How Long Should Each Entry Be?

There is no fixed length. Based on past students, a typical weekly entry is:

- **½ page to 2 pages of text** (not counting images and screenshots)

The length depends on the tasks that week and the depth of your reflections.

---

## 6. Good Practices with Git and Markdown

1. **Write up as you go** — don't wait until the end of the week. Add notes and screenshots right after completing each task.
2. **Edit directly in GitHub** — don't write in Word and plan to transfer later.
3. **Use simple formatting** — headings, lists, links, and embedded images. Don't spend time making it look decorative.
4. **Link between files** — link to your uploaded files and cross-reference other weekly entries when relevant.
5. **Check images display correctly** — view your entry in the GitHub web interface to confirm embedded images appear.
6. **Commit regularly** — commit after each task. If there are 4 tutorial tasks, aim for at least 4 commits during the tutorial, plus more as you finalise your write-up. Multiple commits per week (sometimes tens) are expected.
7. **Returning to fix mistakes** — if you fix a mistake from a previous week, add a reflection explaining what the mistake was and how you identified it.

---

*Good luck with your journal. Write honestly, reflect genuinely, and commit often!*
